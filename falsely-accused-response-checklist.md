# Falsely Accused of Using AI: Response Checklist

If an AI detector has flagged your work and you know you wrote it yourself, this checklist walks you through what to do. It applies to students facing academic misconduct proceedings, freelancers whose work was rejected, and employees facing disciplinary action.

**Important framing:** A detector score is not proof. It is a statistical signal with a measurable error rate. Multiple major tools have independent false positive rates of 14–28%, meaning they incorrectly flag human-written text a significant fraction of the time. The students most likely to be wrongly flagged are non-native English speakers, neurodivergent writers, and people who write in a highly structured style. If you are in one of these groups, say so explicitly in your response.

---

## Phase 1: Gather Evidence Before You Respond

**Step 1 — Request the actual score and tool used**

Do not accept "the AI detector flagged your work" as a sufficient description. Ask:
- Which tool was used?
- What score was returned? (Percentage or category)
- Which passages were highlighted as AI-generated?

You need this information to contest the methodology specifically. A vague "the tool said so" is not enough to act on.

**Step 2 — Document your writing process**

Pull together anything that shows the document evolving over time. The more specific the better.

- [ ] Browser history showing research visits during the writing period
- [ ] Drafts saved in Google Docs version history (File → Version history → See version history)
- [ ] Microsoft Word AutoSave versions or OneDrive edit history
- [ ] Grammarly edit history (Settings → Your writing history)
- [ ] Emails, messages, or comments sent to a tutor, classmate, or colleague about the document while drafting it
- [ ] Notes, outlines, or mind maps you made before writing
- [ ] Screenshots of search results you used as research, with timestamps
- [ ] If you have a writing journal or took notes by hand, photograph them

Even 3–4 of these pieces of evidence taken together create a pattern that is very difficult to fake.

**Step 3 — Run the same text through multiple detectors yourself**

Different detectors return different results for the same text. If GPTZero flags your work and Copyleaks clears it, that is meaningful information about the first tool's limitations on your writing style.

Tools to try (all have free tiers):
- GPTZero (gptzero.me)
- Copyleaks
- Pangram Labs (5 free checks/day)
- Writer.com detector (free)

Save and screenshot all results. Conflicting results across tools demonstrate that the original flag was not definitive.

**Step 4 — Assess whether your writing style is a known false-positive risk factor**

You do not need to prove which risk factor applies. You just need to demonstrate that your writing style has characteristics that AI detectors are documented to misclassify.

Known false-positive risk factors:
- [ ] You are a non-native English speaker (ESL false positive rate documented as high as 61.3% for TOEFL writers in Stanford 2026 research)
- [ ] You write in a highly structured style (academic rubric-following, legal writing, technical/STEM prose)
- [ ] The flagged text is shorter than 300 words (accuracy collapses for all tools below this threshold; vendors acknowledge this)
- [ ] You ran the text through Grammarly, QuillBot's grammar checker, or any paraphrasing tool (these smooth out the "human" irregularities that detectors rely on to clear you)
- [ ] You have a clean, consistent writing style that happens to produce low perplexity text naturally

If any of these apply, include them in your response and cite the relevant evidence (see Step 6).

## Phase 2: Draft Your Response

**Step 5 — Lead with the limitation of the tool, not a protest**

Do not begin your response with "I didn't use AI." Begin with the documented fact that the tool used has a measurable false positive rate and has been independently tested to produce incorrect results on writing profiles like yours.

This reframes the situation: the question is not whether you cheated. The question is whether the tool's output constitutes evidence sufficient to support a formal accusation.

**Step 6 — Cite independent evidence about the tool's accuracy**

If the tool used was one of the commonly used detectors, here is verifiable published data you can cite:

**GPTZero:**
- Independent FPR: 1–18% depending on content type and writer demographics (PCWorld independent test: 62% accuracy overall)
- Stanford Liang 2026 follow-up: 61.3% false positive rate on TOEFL essays by Chinese students

**Turnitin AI Detection:**
- Field false positive rates documented as higher than controlled 1% figure
- Universities that have disabled the tool: Vanderbilt (2023), Curtin University AU (2026), University of Pittsburgh, University of Minnesota, Montclair State University
- Washington Post study: ~50% FPR on a specific content type (Turnitin has disputed but not fully explained this result)

**Originality.ai:**
- Vendor claims 2% FPR; independent testing finds 14–28% FPR
- Scribbr 2026 study: 76% overall accuracy (vs. marketed 99%)

**ZeroGPT:**
- Independent FPR: 14–33% (Supwriter benchmark) — more than 1 in 7 human essays wrongly flagged

**Sapling:**
- 28% FPR in Supwriter benchmark — more than 1 in 4 human writers would be told their work is AI-generated

Cite the original study or benchmark when possible. The Supwriter benchmark and Scribbr 2026 study are publicly accessible.

**Step 7 — Present your process evidence in chronological order**

Lay out your writing process as a timeline. Example structure:

> "I began outlining this essay on [date]. Attached is [Google Docs version history / my draft notes / browser history showing research visits]. On [date], I wrote the first draft, which you can see in [version X] of the document history. I used Grammarly to check grammar on [date], which is a documented cause of false positives in AI detectors because grammar checkers remove the kinds of inconsistencies these tools interpret as human signals. I did not use AI to generate any portion of this text."

**Step 8 — Request a face-to-face or written knowledge demonstration if relevant**

If you are a student, ask to answer questions about the content of your essay in person. A few targeted questions about your argument, the sources you used, or the choices you made in your writing process will demonstrate familiarity that would be impossible to fake if you didn't write it.

## Phase 3: Know Your Formal Rights

**Step 9 — Understand the relevant legal context**

**Students in the US:**
- Under FERPA, your essay content is protected educational data. The institution's use of a third-party AI detector must comply with FERPA data governance requirements.
- Academic misconduct proceedings require substantive evidence, not a single algorithmic score. An AI detector output is not considered legally sufficient alone.
- Request a copy of the institution's AI detection policy in writing. Many institutions have no formal policy, which means the use of these tools may not be authorized under their own academic integrity procedures.

**EU students / EU institutions:**
- GDPR Article 22 restricts automated decision-making with legal effects on individuals. An AI flag that triggers an academic misconduct process likely qualifies. The institution is required to have a human review step in the process.
- EU AI Act Article 50 requires high-risk AI systems to retain logs. Ask the institution what logs they have retained and whether they can demonstrate the tool's false positive rate in your specific context.

**Freelancers and employees:**
- An AI detector score is not a legally recognized form of evidence. In any employment dispute, treat it the same way you would treat any other contested technical evidence — it requires expert context.
- If you signed a contract requiring human-written output, your process documentation (Step 2 above) is the primary defense.

## Quick Reference: What to Gather

| Evidence Type | How to Get It | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Google Docs version history | File → Version history → See version history | Very strong — timestamps cannot be retroactively edited |
| Word AutoSave / OneDrive history | Check OneDrive activity log | Strong |
| Browser history from research period | Export from browser history | Moderate — shows research activity during writing period |
| Grammarly edit history | Grammarly Settings → Your writing history | Moderate — shows you wrote and edited the text |
| Draft notes / outlines (handwritten or digital) | Photograph or screenshot | Moderate |
| Conflicting results from other detectors | Run text through GPTZero, Copyleaks, Pangram, Writer.com | Moderate — demonstrates tool inconsistency |
| ESL status / writing style evidence | Prior essays, transcripts, self-attestation | Moderate — establishes false positive risk factor |
| Email/message thread discussing the work | Export from email | Strong if dated during writing period |
| Face-to-face knowledge demonstration | Request from instructor | Very strong |
